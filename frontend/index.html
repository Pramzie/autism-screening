System Capabilities

What the System
Analyses

Each session captures 50 frames over 5 seconds, running them through a trained CNN alongside five distinct facial feature metrics.

01

Blink Rate Analysis

Measures blink frequency per minute. Atypical rates — below 10 or above 25 per minute — are flagged as potential indicators of ASD.

02

Facial Symmetry

Compares pixel intensity between left and right face halves. Asymmetry exceeding 5% of the normalised mean difference indicates a potential anomaly.

03

Expression Variability

Tracks variance in facial muscle movement between consecutive frames. Values below 50 units suggest reduced emotional expressiveness, a known ASD marker.

04

Head Movement

Tracks displacement of the facial centroid across frames. Both hyperkinetic (above 45px) and hypokinetic (below 2px) motion are flagged as atypical.

05

CNN Classification

All 50 frames are individually scored by the trained Keras model. The averaged output probability determines severity: Non-Autistic, Low, Medium, or High.

06

Severity Banding

Results are mapped to four confidence bands: above 0.85 is High, 0.65–0.84 is Medium, 0.50–0.64 is Low, and below 0.50 is Non-Autistic.

07

Eye Detection Ratio

Measures the proportion of frames in which both eyes are detected via Haar cascade. A ratio below 0.70 may indicate gaze aversion, a documented ASD indicator.

08

Real-Time Feedback

Live blink count, face detection status, symmetry percentage, and elapsed time are displayed on-screen during the 5-second capture window.

Understanding Autism

Awareness &
Education

Autism Spectrum Disorder is a complex neurodevelopmental condition. Early recognition and informed, compassionate support make a meaningful difference in outcomes.

What is ASD

Autism Spectrum Disorder Explained

ASD is a lifelong neurodevelopmental condition characterised by differences in social communication and interaction, alongside restricted and repetitive patterns of behaviour. It is a spectrum; every individual's experience is unique.

Early Detection

Why Early Screening Matters

Research consistently shows that early intervention — ideally before age 3 — leads to significantly better outcomes in language development, social skills, and adaptive behaviour. Accessible screening tools enable earlier identification.

Resources for Parents and Caregivers

Families navigating a diagnosis benefit most from community, professional guidance, and accurate information. Occupational therapy, speech therapy, and structured educational programmes are key pillars of support.

Clinical Indicators

Common Signs
to Watch For

Early signs of ASD may be visible from 12 to 18 months of age. These indicators should prompt consultation with a developmental paediatrician.

Social Communication Differences

Limited or absent eye contact, delayed speech development, difficulty understanding non-literal language, reduced use of gestures, and challenges in back-and-forth conversation. Some children may not respond to their name by 12 months.

Restricted and Repetitive Behaviours

Repetitive motor movements such as hand flapping or rocking, insistence on sameness in routines, highly restricted interests, and unusual sensory responses to light, sound, texture, or smell.

Sensory Processing Differences

Hypersensitivity or hyposensitivity to sensory input is common. This may manifest as distress in noisy environments, aversion to certain textures, reduced pain response, or a strong preference for specific sensory stimuli.

Developmental Milestone Differences

No babbling by 12 months, no meaningful single words by 16 months, no two-word phrases by 24 months, or any regression in previously acquired language or social skills at any age should prompt professional evaluation immediately.

Facial Patterns Detected by AI

Research suggests subtle differences in blink rate, gaze direction, facial symmetry, and expression variability can be detected computationally. This system analyses these patterns non-invasively through a standard webcam feed in real time.
